Introduction

In this analysis we explore some trends that female labour force participation (LFP) has followed since 1980 in OECD countries.
After finding that female LFP has grown at very different rates, and up to various levels, in different regions of the world –being
particularly high in Nordic countries– we use visualization tools to dig deeper into some potential partial explanations.

For example, could public policies inducing the substitution of leisure or housework with market labor -such as childcare-
help explain higher these country-level differences?

First, some simple correlations. Choose a variable to explore how it relates with female LFP (remember: this is merely exploratory!).

Treemap: Daily Time Use Per Gender

So, almost all spending types appear uncorrelated (bunch of scatter clouds) with gender LFP gap - except two: Social benefits/transfers and education

Focusing on the former, if it is the case that higher public social welfare spending incentivizes women to enter (or stay in) the workforce (i.e. public spending
as a "causal mechanism"), then that should be reflected in the way they allocate their time during the day, expecting more even gender distributions
of daily unpaid work in countries with higher social expenditure levels.
(P.S. Unpaid work mainly includes child and elderly care, as well as housework)

As we can explore in the treemap below, there are wide time use differences between genders in "big family-spending" countries and the rest,
with Mexico, Turkey and Japan being at the opposite extreme than the Nordics - in line with their respective social spending distributions.

It is quite clear, though, that, globally, women spend more daily time engaging in unpaid labor and less in paid work, study, and leisure.
